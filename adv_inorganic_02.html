Bridging Group Theory and Quantum Chemistry

1. Symmetry Operations as Linear Transformations

In quantum mechanics, a symmetry operation $\hat{R}$ acts on a wavefunction $\psi$. This action can be mapped to a matrix multiplication if we define a suitable basis set.

$$ \hat{R} \phi_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \Gamma(R)_{ji} \phi_j $$

The set of matrices $\Gamma(R)$ for all operations in a group is called a Representation of that group.

2. Similarity Transformations

The specific form of a matrix $\Gamma(R)$ depends on the choice of basis vectors. We can switch between different bases using a Similarity Transformation.

$$ \Gamma'(R) = P^{-1} \Gamma(R) P $$

Important Property: Trace Invariance

The Trace (sum of diagonals) of a matrix is invariant under similarity transformations. Thus, the Character ($\chi$) of an operation is independent of the basis.

3. Decomposition of Representations

A Reducible Representation ($\Gamma_{red}$) can be block-diagonalized into Irreducible Representations ($\Gamma_i$).

$$ \Gamma_{red}(R) = \begin{pmatrix} \Gamma_1(R) & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \Gamma_2(R) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \dots \end{pmatrix} $$

Characters obey the same additive rule:

$$ \chi_{red}(R) = \sum_{i} n_i \chi_i(R) $$

4. The Great Orthogonality Theorem (GOT)

The most powerful tool in algebraic symmetry analysis.

$$ \sum_{R} \left[ \Gamma_i(R)_{mn} \right]^* \left[ \Gamma_j(R)_{m'n'} \right] = \frac{h}{\sqrt{l_i l_j}} \delta_{ij} \delta_{mm'} \delta_{nn'} $$

h: Order of the group (Total ops)
l: Dimension of the IR

$\delta$: Kronecker Delta (Selects orthogonal components)

5. Derived Rules from GOT

GOT leads to 5 fundamental rules for Character Table construction.

  • Rule 1: Dimensionality

    $\sum_i l_i^2 = h$

    The sum of the squares of the dimensions of the IRs equals the order of the group.

  • Rule 2: Trace Orthogonality

    $\sum_R [\chi_i(R)]^2 = h$

    The characters of any IR behave like an orthogonal vector in $h$-dimensional space.

    • 6. Derived Rules (Cont.)

    Rule 3: Row Orthogonality

    $\sum_R \chi_i(R) \chi_j(R) = 0$ (for $i \neq j$)

    Different IRs are strictly orthogonal. Their dot product is zero.

    Rule 4: Number of IRs

    Number of IRs = Number of Classes

    This ensures character tables are always square matrices.

    7. The Reduction Formula (Decomposition)

    How to find exactly how many times an IR ($\Gamma_i$) appears in a reducible representation ($\Gamma_{red}$):

    $$ n_i = \frac{1}{h} \sum_c g_c \chi_i(c) \chi_{red}(c) $$

    Essential for: IR/Raman peaks & Hybridization

    8. Mulliken Symbols: Decoding the Nomenclature

    A vs B (Dimension = 1)

    A: Symmetric (+1) to Principal Axis.
    B: Antisymmetric (-1) to Principal Axis.

    E (Dim = 2) & T (Dim = 3)

    Indicate energy degeneracy. Mixed by rotation operations.

    Subscripts g/u (Parity)

    g (gerade): Symmetric to $i$.
    u (ungerade): Antisymmetric to $i$.

    Prime (') and Double Prime ('')

    ': Symmetric to $\sigma_h$.
    '': Antisymmetric to $\sigma_h$.

    9. Strategy for Table Construction

    When solving for unknown characters, follow this hierarchical logic:

    1. Determine $h$ and number of classes.
    2. Assign $l_i$ using $\sum l_i^2 = h$.
    3. Fill the Totally Symmetric row (always all +1s).
    4. Apply Rule 3 (Orthogonality) to find remaining integers.
    5. For non-Abelian groups, use $\chi(C_n) = 1 + 2\cos\theta$ for the E representation.

    10. Construction Challenge: $C_{3v}$

    $C_{3v} = \{ E, 2C_3, 3\sigma_v \} \implies h = 6$, 3 Classes.

    $$ 1^2 + 1^2 + 2^2 = 6 \implies \text{IRs: } A_1, A_2, E $$
    $C_{3v}$$E$$2C_3$$3\sigma_v$
    $A_1$111
    $A_2$1??
    $E$2??

    11. Solving $C_{3v}$ via Orthogonality

    Solve $A_2$ characters (x, y):

    $A_1 \cdot A_2 = (1)(1) + 2(1)x + 3(1)y = 0 \implies 1 + 2x + 3y = 0$

    Rule 2: $1^2 + 2x^2 + 3y^2 = 6$

    $\implies x = 1, y = -1$. (Matches $A_2$ definition)

    Solve $E$ characters (a, b):

    $A_1 \cdot E = (1)(2) + 2(1)a + 3(1)b = 0 \implies 2 + 2a + 3b = 0$

    $A_2 \cdot E = (1)(2) + 2(1)a + 3(-1)b = 0 \implies 2 + 2a - 3b = 0$

    $\implies a = -1, b = 0$.

    12. The Completed $C_{3v}$ Character Table

    $C_{3v}$$E$$2C_3$$3\sigma_v$
    $A_1$111
    $A_2$11-1
    $E$2-10

    Fundamental for $NH_3$ and Pyramidal Complexes

    13. Functions in the Table (x, y, z)

    The rightmost columns of a Character Table list functions that transform according to that IR.

    Linear Functions

    $(x, y, z)$ and $(R_x, R_y, R_z)$. These define Infrared Activity and Translational Motion.

    Quadratic Functions

    $(x^2, xy, \dots)$. These define Raman Activity and the symmetry of d-orbitals.
