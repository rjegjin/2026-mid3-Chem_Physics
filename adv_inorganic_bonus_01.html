Chapter 01 | Slide 2

The Algebraic Structure: "Group"

Symmetry is not merely a visual intuition; it is strictly defined by an algebraic structure known as a Group.

Axioms of a Group $(G, \cdot)$
  • Closure: $\forall a, b \in G$, $a \cdot b \in G$
  • Associativity: $\forall a, b, c \in G$, $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$
  • Identity: $\exists e \in G$ such that $a \cdot e = e \cdot a = a$
  • Inverse: $\forall a \in G$, $\exists a^{-1} \in G$ such that $a \cdot a^{-1} = e$
Chapter 01 | Slide 3

Subgroups

When a subset $H$ of a group $G$ satisfies the group axioms under the same operation, $H$ is called a subgroup of $G$ ($H \leq G$).

Subgroup Test

The necessary and sufficient condition for $H$ to be a subgroup is summarized in one line:

\\[ \forall a, b \in H \implies a \cdot b^{-1} \in H \\]

Example: Within the $C_{3v}$ molecular point group, there exists a subgroup $C_3 = \{E, C_3, C_3^2\}$ consisting solely of rotational operations.

Chapter 01 | Slide 4

Cosets

We can use a subgroup $H$ to partition the entire group $G$ into non-overlapping regions.

The Left Coset of $H$ with respect to an element $g \in G$:

\\[ gH = \{g \cdot h \mid h \in H\} \\]

Property: Two cosets are either identical or completely disjoint ($aH \cap bH = \emptyset$). Therefore, cosets form a perfect Partition of $G$.

Chapter 01 | Slide 5

Lagrange's Theorem

Lagrange's Theorem

For any finite group $G$ and subgroup $H$, the order (number of elements) of $H$, $|H|$, must perfectly divide the order of $G$, $|G|$.

\\[ |G| = [G:H] \cdot |H| \\]

Proof Sketch: Every left coset $gH$ has exactly the same number of elements as $H$ (via the bijection $f: h \mapsto gh$). Since $G$ is the union of these disjoint cosets, the total number of elements in $G$ must be a multiple of $|H|$.

Chapter 01 | Slide 6

Conjugacy Classes

This is the algebraic definition of a Class, which groups symmetry operations in Character Tables.

For two elements $a, b \in G$, if there exists an $x \in G$ such that:

\\[ b = x^{-1} \cdot a \cdot x \\]

Then we say "$a$ and $b$ are conjugate to each other."

Chapter 01 | Slide 7

Equivalence Relations

Conjugacy mathematically forms an Equivalence Relation.

  • Reflexive: $a = e^{-1} a e$ (An element is conjugate to itself)
  • Symmetric: $b = x^{-1}ax \implies a = (x^{-1})^{-1}bx^{-1}$
  • Transitive: $b = x^{-1}ax, \text{ and } c = y^{-1}by \implies c = (xy)^{-1}a(xy)$

Conclusion: The entire group $G$ perfectly fractures into disjoint Conjugacy Classes.

Chapter 01 | Slide 8

Normal Subgroups

A special subgroup where the left coset equals the right coset for every $g \in G$.

\\[ gH = Hg \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad g^{-1}Hg = H \quad \forall g \in G \\]

A normal subgroup $H$ is an invariant space under the conjugacy operation of $G$. This becomes the core tool for "dividing" a group into smaller structural components.

Chapter 01 | Slide 9

Quotient Groups

When $H$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, we can construct a new group where the elements are the cosets themselves.

Quotient Group $G/H$

Operation Definition: $(aH) \cdot (bH) = (ab)H$

This operation is only well-defined if and only if $H$ is a normal subgroup.

Chapter 01 | Slide 10

Isomorphisms

Two groups $G$ and $G'$ may look different (different letters, different geometric actions), but possess the exact same mathematical structure.

A bijection $\phi: G \to G'$ is an isomorphism ($G \cong G'$) if:

\\[ \phi(a \cdot_G b) = \phi(a) \cdot_{G'} \phi(b) \\]

Note: By Cayley's Theorem, every finite point group is essentially isomorphic to a subgroup of a symmetric group.

Chapter 02 | Slide 11

Hilbert Space ($\mathcal{H}$)

In quantum mechanics, a wavefunction $\psi$ is an element (a 'Ket', $|\psi\rangle$) residing in an infinite-dimensional complex vector space.

Inner Product Axioms $\langle \phi | \psi \rangle$
  • $\langle \phi | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi | \phi \rangle^*$ (Conjugate symmetry)
  • $\langle \phi | a\psi_1 + b\psi_2 \rangle = a\langle \phi | \psi_1 \rangle + b\langle \phi | \psi_2 \rangle$ (Linearity in ket)
  • $\langle \psi | \psi \rangle \geq 0$ (Equals 0 iff $|\psi\rangle = 0$)
Chapter 02 | Slide 12

Linear Operators

A symmetry operation $\hat{R}$ is a linear mapping that moves points within this Hilbert space to other points.

\\[ \hat{R}(c_1|\psi_1\rangle + c_2|\psi_2\rangle) = c_1\hat{R}|\psi_1\rangle + c_2\hat{R}|\psi_2\rangle \\]

Because of this Linearity, we do not need to track infinitely many states. We only need to know how a finite set of Basis Vectors transforms to completely define the operator.

Chapter 02 | Slide 13

Matrix Representations

Given an orthonormal basis $\{|e_i\rangle\}$, the action of an operator $\hat{R}$ is perfectly described by a matrix.

\\[ \hat{R} |e_j\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n \Gamma(R)_{ij} |e_i\rangle \\]

Deriving Matrix Elements via Bra-Ket:

\\[ \Gamma(R)_{ij} = \langle e_i | \hat{R} | e_j \rangle \\]
Chapter 02 | Slide 14

Adjoint Operators ($\hat{R}^\dagger$)

The most important symmetric partner of an operator in physics.

The adjoint $\hat{R}^\dagger$ is defined such that for any $|\phi\rangle, |\psi\rangle$:

\\[ \langle \phi | \hat{R} \psi \rangle = \langle \hat{R}^\dagger \phi | \psi \rangle \\]

In matrix notation, this corresponds to the Conjugate Transpose: $\Gamma_{ij}^\dagger = \Gamma_{ji}^*$.

Chapter 02 | Slide 15

Unitary Operators

Physical symmetry operations must preserve the 'magnitude' (total probability = 1) and 'angles' (relative phase) of states.

\\[ \langle \hat{R}\phi | \hat{R}\psi \rangle = \langle \phi | \hat{R}^\dagger \hat{R} | \psi \rangle = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \\]

$\implies \hat{R}^\dagger \hat{R} = \hat{I} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{R}^\dagger = \hat{R}^{-1}$

Therefore, the matrix representation of any physical symmetry operation is always a Unitary Matrix.

Chapter 02 | Slide 16

Hermitian Operators

An operator that is equal to its own adjoint ($\hat{H}^\dagger = \hat{H}$). In quantum mechanics, all Observables (e.g., Energy, Momentum) are Hermitian operators.

The Spectral Theorem
  • The eigenvalues of a Hermitian operator are always real numbers.
  • Eigenvectors corresponding to different eigenvalues are strictly orthogonal.
Chapter 02 | Slide 17

Change of Basis

The exact same symmetry operation will look like a different matrix depending on the coordinate system (basis) the observer chooses.

When shifting to a new basis $|f_i\rangle = \sum P_{ji} |e_j\rangle$, the matrix representation transforms as:

\\[ \Gamma'(R) = P^{-1} \Gamma(R) P \\]

This is defined as a Similarity Transformation.

Chapter 02 | Slide 18

Invariance of the Trace

Certain properties of matrices survive similarity transformations unscathed. The most crucial one is the Trace (sum of diagonals).

\\[ \text{Tr}(P^{-1} \Gamma P) = \text{Tr}(P P^{-1} \Gamma) = \text{Tr}(\Gamma) \\]

In Group Theory, this invariant trace is called the Character ($\chi$). It is a pure mathematical invariant, completely liberated from the arbitrary choice of basis vectors.

Chapter 02 | Slide 19

Direct Sum ($\oplus$)

Combining two vector spaces $V$ and $W$ to create a larger space $V \oplus W$.

\\[ \Gamma_{V \oplus W} = \begin{pmatrix} \Gamma_V & 0 \\ 0 & \Gamma_W \end{pmatrix} \\]

From a matrix perspective, this forms a Block Diagonal structure. The characters sum trivially: $\chi_{V \oplus W} = \chi_V + \chi_W$.

Chapter 02 | Slide 20

Tensor Product ($\otimes$)

A more complex union where dimensions multiply. Essential for describing multi-electron interactions.

\\[ A \otimes B = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11}B & a_{12}B & \cdots \\ a_{21}B & a_{22}B & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{pmatrix} \\]

Crucial Theorem: The trace of a tensor product matrix is the product of the individual traces.

\\[ \chi_{V \otimes W}(R) = \chi_V(R) \cdot \chi_W(R) \\]
Chapter 03 | Slide 21

Mathematical Definition of a Representation

A function that maps each element $g$ of a group $G$ to a matrix $\Gamma(g)$ in the General Linear group $GL(V)$, while preserving the group structure.

Homomorphism Condition \\[ \Gamma(g_1 \cdot g_2) = \Gamma(g_1) \Gamma(g_2) \quad \forall g_1, g_2 \in G \\] \\[ \Gamma(e) = I, \quad \Gamma(g^{-1}) = \Gamma(g)^{-1} \\]
Chapter 03 | Slide 22

Faithful Representations

If different elements of the group map strictly to different matrices (a 1:1 mapping), the representation is called 'Faithful'.

Injective function condition:

\\[ \Gamma(g_1) = \Gamma(g_2) \implies g_1 = g_2 \\]

Example: The 'Totally Symmetric Representation' ($A_1$), where every element maps to the number 1, is trivial and unfaithful.

Chapter 03 | Slide 23

Equivalent Representations

Two representations $\Gamma^{(1)}$ and $\Gamma^{(2)}$ of the same dimension are 'Equivalent' if there exists a fixed invertible matrix $P$ such that:

\\[ \Gamma^{(1)}(g) = P^{-1} \Gamma^{(2)}(g) P \quad \forall g \in G \\]

Physically, this implies they represent the exact same physical system, merely viewed from a different coordinate system.

Chapter 03 | Slide 24

Invariant Subspaces

A subspace $W$ of the total vector space $V$ is invariant if symmetry operations never push vectors outside of $W$.

\\[ \forall g \in G, \quad \vec{w} \in W \implies \Gamma(g)\vec{w} \in W \\]

Example: If the $p_z$ orbital in a molecule never mixes with $x$ or $y$ orbitals under any symmetry operation, the 1D space spanned by $p_z$ is an invariant subspace.

Chapter 03 | Slide 25

Reducibility

If a non-trivial invariant subspace $W$ exists (neither $\{0\}$ nor $V$), the representation is defined as a Reducible Representation.

Through a suitable basis change $P$, all matrices $\Gamma(g)$ can be brought into a block upper-triangular form:

\\[ P^{-1} \Gamma(g) P = \begin{pmatrix} \Gamma_1(g) & M(g) \\ 0 & \Gamma_2(g) \end{pmatrix} \\]
Chapter 03 | Slide 26

Complete Reducibility

For unitary representations of finite groups, if $W$ is invariant, its orthogonal complement $W^\perp$ is also invariant.

Maschke's Theorem

Every finite-dimensional representation of a finite group can be completely decomposed into a Direct Sum of Irreducible Representations.

\\[ \Gamma(g) \sim \begin{pmatrix} \Gamma_1(g) & 0 \\ 0 & \Gamma_2(g) \end{pmatrix} \implies \Gamma = \Gamma_1 \oplus \Gamma_2 \\]
Chapter 03 | Slide 27

Irreducible Representation (IR)

The Fundamental Building Blocks

A representation that possesses NO non-trivial invariant subspaces. It cannot be broken down any further.

IRs define the minimum allowable spatial dimensions permitted by nature's symmetry. In particle physics, they classify elementary particles. In chemistry, they represent the degenerate energy levels themselves.

Chapter 03 | Slide 28

Restriction on the Number of IRs

A finite group $G$ cannot have an infinite number of non-equivalent irreducible representations.

Number of IRs = Number of Conjugacy Classes

Because of this theorem, the Character Table is always forced to be a Square Matrix, with the number of rows (IRs) exactly matching the number of columns (Classes).

Chapter 03 | Slide 29

The Special Case of 1D IRs

For Abelian groups (where commutative law holds), ALL irreducible representations are strictly 1-Dimensional.

If operations commute ($ab = ba$), their matrices must commute: $\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b) = \Gamma(b)\Gamma(a)$.

By Linear Algebra, a set of commuting unitary matrices can be Simultaneously Diagonalized.

Being fully diagonalized means the space fractures into 1D blocks. Thus, no IR can be larger than 1D.

Chapter 04 | Slide 31

Entering Schur's Lemmas

These two lemmas act as the Mathematical Bridge connecting basic group axioms to the Great Orthogonality Theorem (GOT). Proven by Issai Schur in 1905.

"Without these lemmas, we could not compute a single number inside any Character Table."

Chapter 04 | Slide 32

Schur's Lemma I: The Proposition

Schur's Lemma I

Let $\Gamma(g)$ be an Irreducible Representation over a complex vector space $V$. If a linear operator $M$ commutes with $\Gamma(g)$ for all $g \in G$:

\\[ M \Gamma(g) = \Gamma(g) M \quad \forall g \in G \\]

Then $M$ MUST be a scalar multiple of the identity matrix.

\\[ M = c \cdot I \quad (c \in \mathbb{C}) \\]
Chapter 04 | Slide 33

Schur's Lemma I: Proof Part 1

Over the field of complex numbers, any matrix $M$ must have at least one eigenvalue $\lambda$ and corresponding eigenvector $\vec{v}$ ($M\vec{v} = \lambda\vec{v}$).

Let us define a new matrix: $M - \lambda I$.
The null space (kernel, $N$) of this matrix is non-trivial because $\vec{v} \in N$.

\\[ \forall \vec{w} \in N, \quad (M - \lambda I)\vec{w} = \vec{0} \\]
Chapter 04 | Slide 34

Schur's Lemma I: Proof Part 2

Now, apply $\Gamma(g)$ to any arbitrary vector $\vec{w} \in N$.

\begin{align} (M - \lambda I) \Gamma(g) \vec{w} &= (M \Gamma(g) - \lambda \Gamma(g)) \vec{w} \\ &= (\Gamma(g) M - \Gamma(g) \lambda I) \vec{w} \quad (\because M \text{ commutes with } \Gamma) \\ &= \Gamma(g) (M - \lambda I) \vec{w} \\ &= \Gamma(g) \vec{0} = \vec{0} \end{align}

This proves that $\Gamma(g)\vec{w}$ is also in the kernel $N$.
Thus, the kernel $N$ is an Invariant Subspace under $\Gamma$!

Chapter 04 | Slide 35

Schur's Lemma I: Conclusion

But recall our premise: $\Gamma$ is an Irreducible Representation. IRs cannot possess non-trivial invariant subspaces.

Therefore, the kernel $N$ must be either $\{\vec{0}\}$ or the entire space $V$.

Since we already established that the eigenvector $\vec{v} \neq \vec{0}$ resides in $N$, $N$ cannot be zero. It must be the whole space $V$.

Since $(M - \lambda I)\vec{x} = \vec{0}$ for all $\vec{x}$,
$M = \lambda I$ $\quad \blacksquare$

Chapter 04 | Slide 36

Schur's Lemma II: The Proposition

Schur's Lemma II

Let $\Gamma^{(1)}$ (dim $l_1$) and $\Gamma^{(2)}$ (dim $l_2$) be two IRs. If a matrix $M$ satisfies the following relation:

\\[ M \Gamma^{(1)}(g) = \Gamma^{(2)}(g) M \quad \forall g \in G \\]

Then, ONLY ONE of the following is true:

  1. $M = 0$ (The zero matrix)
  2. $\Gamma^{(1)}$ and $\Gamma^{(2)}$ are equivalent ($l_1 = l_2$), and $M$ is an invertible matrix.
Chapter 04 | Slide 37

Schur's Lemma II: Proof Structure

This proof also hinges on the logic of invariant subspaces.

It can be shown that:

  • The Kernel of matrix $M$ forms an invariant subspace in the domain of $\Gamma^{(1)}$.
  • The Image (range) of matrix $M$ forms an invariant subspace in the domain of $\Gamma^{(2)}$.

Because both representations are Irreducible, these spaces must force an all-or-nothing scenario (either zero or full space), directly proving the proposition.

Chapter 04 | Slide 38

From Pure Math to Physics

Schur’s Lemmas are products of pure algebra, but when applied to quantum mechanics, the implications are profound.

"If the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$ commutes with all symmetry operators $\hat{R}$ ($[\hat{H}, \hat{R}]=0$), then by Schur's Lemma I, within the space of a specific Irreducible Representation, $\hat{H}$ must act as a constant matrix ($E \cdot I$)."

This is the fundamental origin of Energy Degeneracy.

Chapter 05 | Slide 41

Unveiling the GOT

Armed with Schur's Lemmas, we can now prove the Great Orthogonality Theorem (GOT), the bridge connecting math to physical observables.

The Great Orthogonality Theorem
\\[ \sum_{R} \Gamma_i(R)_{mn}^* \Gamma_j(R)_{m'n'} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{l_i l_j}} \delta_{ij} \delta_{mm'} \delta_{nn'} \\]

This equation states that the individual matrix elements of Irreducible Representations act as mutually orthogonal vectors in an $h$-dimensional space.

Chapter 05 | Slide 42

GOT Proof 1: Constructing Invariance

The crux of the proof involves artificially creating an Invariant Matrix by averaging over all group operations.

For any arbitrary matrix $X$, construct matrix $M$ as follows:

\\[ M = \sum_{R} \Gamma_i(R) X \Gamma_j(R^{-1}) \\]

Through group rearrangement, one can prove that this matrix $M$ satisfies $\Gamma_i(S) M = M \Gamma_j(S)$ for any operation $S \in G$!

Chapter 05 | Slide 43

GOT Proof 2: Invoking Schur

We now apply Schur's Lemma II to this engineered matrix $M$.

If $i \neq j$ (they are non-equivalent IRs), Lemma II dictates that $M$ MUST be $0$.

\\[ \sum_{R} \Gamma_i(R) X \Gamma_j(R^{-1}) = 0 \\]

If we set the arbitrary matrix $X$ such that only one element $X_{pq}=1$ and the rest are 0, this immediately derives the $\delta_{ij}$ (orthogonality between different representations) part of the GOT.

Chapter 05 | Slide 44

Invariance of Character (Class Function)

Let's revisit the property of the Trace ($\chi$).

If two operations $A$ and $B$ belong to the same Conjugacy Class, then $B = X^{-1} A X$.

\begin{align} \chi(B) &= \text{Tr}(\Gamma(B)) = \text{Tr}(\Gamma(X^{-1} A X)) \\ &= \text{Tr}(\Gamma(X)^{-1} \Gamma(A) \Gamma(X)) \\ &= \text{Tr}(\Gamma(X) \Gamma(X)^{-1} \Gamma(A)) \quad (\because \text{Cyclic property of Trace})\\ &= \text{Tr}(\Gamma(A)) = \chi(A) \end{align}

Conclusion: Operations in the same class ALWAYS share the exact same character.

Chapter 05 | Slide 45

Character Orthogonality

By taking only the diagonal elements ($m=n, m'=n'$) from the GOT equation and summing them up ($\text{Trace}$):

\\[ \sum_{R} \chi_i(R)^* \chi_j(R) = h \delta_{ij} \\]

Rewriting this using the number of elements in each class ($g_c$), we yield the famous Rule 3 of Character Table construction:

\\[ \sum_{c} g_c \chi_i(c)^* \chi_j(c) = h \delta_{ij} \\]
Chapter 06 | Slide 51

The Projection Operator

Mathematically, a Projection Operator maps the entire vector space $V$ onto a specific target subspace $W$. It must satisfy idempotency ($\hat{P}^2 = \hat{P}$).

Character Projection Operator \\[ \hat{P}^{(i)} = \frac{l_i}{h} \sum_{R} \chi^{(i)}(R)^* \hat{R} \\]

Applying this operator to any arbitrary function $f$ systematically strips away all parts except those conforming exactly to the symmetry of the $i$-th Irreducible Representation, yielding the SALC.

Chapter 06 | Slide 52

Tensor Product Spaces

When dealing with multi-electron systems or Polarizability tensors, we must use the Kronecker Product.

\\[ A \otimes B = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11}B & a_{12}B & \cdots \\ a_{21}B & a_{22}B & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{pmatrix} \\]

Trace Property: $\chi_{A \otimes B} = \chi_A \cdot \chi_B$

Chapter 06 | Slide 53

Decomposition of Direct Products

The tensor product of two IRs, $\Gamma_i \otimes \Gamma_j$, is generally a Reducible Representation.

We use the reduction formula to decompose it into a direct sum of IRs (Clebsch-Gordan series):

\\[ \Gamma_i \otimes \Gamma_j = \sum_{k} c_{ij}^k \Gamma_k \\]

The process of finding the coefficients $c_{ij}^k$ is mathematically identical to the Addition of Angular Momentum in quantum mechanics.

Chapter 07 | Slide 61

Symmetry of the Hamiltonian

Saying a physical system is invariant under an operation $\hat{R}$ means the potential energy configuration is unaffected by that operation.

\\[ \hat{R}^\dagger \hat{H} \hat{R} = \hat{H} \iff [\hat{H}, \hat{R}] = 0 \\]

This Commutativity algebraically guarantees that the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$ and the symmetry operator $\hat{R}$ can share a set of Simultaneous Eigenfunctions.

Chapter 07 | Slide 62

The Group Theoretic Origin of Degeneracy

Let $\psi_n$ be a solution to the Schrödinger equation $\hat{H}\psi_n = E\psi_n$. Apply a symmetry operation $\hat{R}$ to it:

\\[ \hat{H}(\hat{R}\psi_n) = \hat{R}\hat{H}\psi_n = \hat{R}(E\psi_n) = E(\hat{R}\psi_n) \\]

Thus, $\hat{R}\psi_n$ is also a valid eigenfunction with the exact same energy $E$.

Conclusion: All wavefunctions belonging to the energy level $E$ form an invariant subspace under group $G$. This subspace is an Irreducible Representation. The dimension ($l_i$) of this IR is the exact Degeneracy of the energy level!

Chapter 08 | Slide 71

The Vanishing Integral Theorem

Consider an integral over all space: $I = \int f(x,y,z) d\tau$.

If the integrand $f$ does not contain the Totally Symmetric Representation ($A_1$ or $A_g$) of the group, the value of this integral is mathematically, rigorously Zero.

Reasoning: Lacking total symmetry implies the function changes sign under certain operations (e.g., inversion). Integrating across symmetric space guarantees perfect cancellation of positive and negative regions.

Chapter 08 | Slide 72

Transition Probability & Selection Rules

The probability of an electronic transition via photon absorption is proportional to the transition dipole moment integral.

\\[ I = \langle \psi_{final} | \hat{\mu} | \psi_{initial} \rangle = \int \psi_{final}^* \hat{\mu} \psi_{initial} d\tau \\]

For this integral to be non-zero (an Allowed Transition), the direct product of the symmetries of the initial state, the dipole operator, and the final state must span $A_1$.

\\[ \Gamma(\psi_f) \otimes \Gamma(\mu) \otimes \Gamma(\psi_i) \supset A_1 \\]

Epilogue: Previewing Wigner-Eckart

The Pinnacle of Symmetry

The Wigner-Eckart Theorem \\[ \langle \alpha, j, m | T_q^{(k)} | \alpha', j', m' \rangle = \langle j' k ; m' q | j' k ; j m \rangle \frac{\langle \alpha, j || T^{(k)} || \alpha', j' \rangle}{\sqrt{2j+1}} \\]

"Group theory allows us to factorize the integral of a physical interaction into a pure geometric component (Clebsch-Gordan coefficient) and a dynamical component (Reduced matrix element). We evaluate the former to bypass the intractable complexities of the latter."
