Lecture 04: The Projection Operator Method and MO Diagram Construction

1. Review: LCAO-MO Theory

Molecular Orbitals ($\Psi$) are formed by the Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals ($\phi$).

$$ \Psi = c_1 \phi_1 + c_2 \phi_2 + \dots + c_n \phi_n $$

Two Primary Conditions for Interaction

  • ● Energy Proximity: Atomic orbitals must have comparable energies.
  • ● Symmetry Match: The integral $\langle \phi_i | \hat{H} | \phi_j \rangle$ is non-zero ONLY if both orbitals belong to the same IR.

2. Symmetry-Adapted Linear Combinations (SALCs)

In multi-center systems, we group equivalent atomic orbitals into **Basis Sets** that transform according to the point group of the molecule.

The SALC Goal

Instead of individual atomic orbitals, we use their linear combinations that match the IRs of the group. This decouples the secular determinant into smaller, manageable blocks.

Why SALCs?

Symmetry ensures that an orbital of $A_1$ symmetry cannot interact with an orbital of $B_1$ symmetry. SALCs automatically group orbitals by their interaction potential.

3. The Projection Operator Method

How do we mathematically derive SALCs? We use the Projection Operator ($\hat{P}$).

$$ \hat{P}^{\Gamma_i} \phi = \frac{l_i}{h} \sum_R \chi_i(R) \hat{R} \phi $$

1. Start with one basis orbital (e.g., $\phi_1$).

2. Apply all symmetry operations ($\hat{R}$) of the point group to it.

3. Multiply each result by the corresponding character ($\chi_i$) from the table.

4. Sum them up to get the SALC for representation $\Gamma_i$.

6. Central Atom Orbitals (Oxygen)

Consult the $C_{2v}$ Character Table for valence orbitals of Oxygen ($2s, 2p$):

  • $2s$ Orbital: $A_1$
  • $2p_z$ Orbital: $A_1$
  • $2p_x$ Orbital: $B_1$
  • $2p_y$ Orbital: $B_2$

Interactions possible with H SALCs ($A_1, B_2$):

  • $A_1$ (H SALC) + $2s, 2p_z$ (O)
  • $B_2$ (H SALC) + $2p_y$ (O)
  • $2p_x$ (O) remains Non-Bonding ($B_1$)

7. Constructing the MO Diagram

  1. Determine Point Group ($C_{2v}$).
  2. Find $\Gamma_{basis}$ for terminal atoms (SALCs).
  3. Decompose $\Gamma_{basis}$ into IRs.
  4. Identify symmetry of central atom orbitals.
  5. Combine orbitals of the SAME symmetry.
  6. Rank levels by node count and energy.

8. Advanced: $O_h$ $\sigma$-bonding

In transition metal complexes (e.g., $[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}$), we use the 6 ligand lone pair vectors as basis set.

$$ \Gamma_{\sigma} = A_{1g} + E_g + T_{1u} $$

Matching Metal d-orbitals in $O_h$:

  • $s$ orbital: $A_{1g}$ (Match!)
  • $p$ orbitals: $T_{1u}$ (Match!)
  • $d$ orbitals ($d_{z^2}, d_{x^2-y^2}$): $E_g$ (Match!)
  • $d$ orbitals ($d_{xy}, d_{yz}, d_{zx}$): $T_{2g}$ (Non-Bonding!)

9. Transition to Ligand Field Theory

The splitting of d-orbitals in a complex is a direct result of these symmetry interactions.

$\sigma$-Bonding

Increases the energy of $e_g$ orbitals (Stronger overlap).

$\pi$-Bonding

Affects the $t_{2g}$ orbitals. Can be donor (weak field) or acceptor (strong field).
