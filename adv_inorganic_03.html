Lecture 03: Normal Modes, Selection Rules, and Mutual Exclusion

1. Concept: Normal Modes

Molecular vibrations are complex, but can be decomposed into independent basic motions.

Mathematical Definition

A normal mode is a vibration where all atoms move with the same frequency and in phase, but with different amplitudes.

The Power of Symmetry

Group Theory allows us to predict the number, symmetry, and spectroscopic activity of these modes without solving the differential equations of motion.

2. Degrees of Freedom

For a molecule with $N$ atoms, there are $3N$ total degrees of freedom.

Non-Linear Molecules

  • Translation: 3 ($x, y, z$)
  • Rotation: 3 ($R_x, R_y, R_z$)
  • Vibration: $3N - 6$

Linear Molecules

  • Translation: 3 ($x, y, z$)
  • Rotation: 2 (Axis rotation is null)
  • Vibration: $3N - 5$

3. Deriving $\Gamma_{3N}$

We use the $3N$ Cartesian coordinates as our basis to find the reducible representation of the total motion.

$$ \chi_{3N}(R) = (\text{Number of Unshifted Atoms}) \times \chi_{xyz}(R) $$

Rule of Thumb

Atoms that shift to a new position contribute 0 to the trace. Only atoms that stay in place (or map to themselves) contribute.

Reminder: $\chi_{xyz}$ values

$E=3, i=-3, \sigma=1, C_2=-1$

4. Case Study: $H_2O$ ($C_{2v}$)

Calculating the total motion characters for a 3-atom system.

$C_{2v}$$E$$C_2$$\sigma_v(xz)$$\sigma_v'(yz)$
Unshifted Atoms31 (O)31 (O)
$\chi_{xyz}$3-111
$\Gamma_{3N}$9-131

Total degrees of freedom: $3 \times 3 = 9$. Match verified.

5. Decomposition of Total Motion

Using the reduction formula to find the symmetry species involved:

$$ \Gamma_{3N} = 3A_1 + A_2 + 3B_1 + 2B_2 $$

Total motions include Translation and Rotation. We must subtract them to find pure vibrations.

$\Gamma_{trans} (x, y, z)$

$A_1 + B_1 + B_2$

$\Gamma_{rot} (R_x, R_y, R_z)$

$A_2 + B_1 + B_2$

6. Extracting Pure Vibrations ($\Gamma_{vib}$)

$$ \Gamma_{vib} = \Gamma_{3N} - (\Gamma_{trans} + \Gamma_{rot}) $$

Result for Water

$\Gamma_{vib} = 2A_1 + B_1$

Number of modes: $2 + 1 = 3$. Consistent with $3N-6 = 3$.

7. Selection Rules

Which modes will appear in the spectrum?

Infrared (IR) Active

Vibration must change the Dipole Moment ($\mu$).

$\implies$ Mode symmetry must match $(x, y, z)$ in the table.

Raman Active

Vibration must change the Polarizability ($\alpha$).

$\implies$ Mode symmetry must match quadratic functions $(x^2, xy, \text{etc.})$

8. Spectral Prediction for $H_2O$

Check the $C_{2v}$ Character Table for $2A_1 + B_1$ modes:

$A_1$ Modes (2)

  • ● IR: Matches $z$ ACTIVE
  • ● Raman: Matches $x^2, y^2, z^2$ ACTIVE

$B_1$ Mode (1)

  • ● IR: Matches $x$ ACTIVE
  • ● Raman: Matches $xz$ ACTIVE

Total Predicted: 3 IR Peaks, 3 Raman Peaks.

9. Rule of Mutual Exclusion

"For molecules with a center of symmetry ($i$), no mode can be both IR and Raman active."

IR Active ($u$)

Linear functions $(x,y,z)$ always have ungerade symmetry ($u$).

Raman Active ($g$)

Quadratic functions always have gerade symmetry ($g$).

10. Case Study: $CO_2$ ($D_{\infty h}$)

Total Modes: $3(3) - 5 = 4$.

  • Symmetric Stretch ($\Sigma_g^+$)

    Gerade ($g$) $\rightarrow$ Raman ACTIVE, IR Inactive

  • Asymmetric Stretch ($\Sigma_u^+$)

    Ungerade ($u$) $\rightarrow$ IR ACTIVE, Raman Inactive

  • Bending ($\Pi_u$, doubly degenerate)

    Ungerade ($u$) $\rightarrow$ IR ACTIVE, Raman Inactive

11. Stretching Only Analysis

In complex molecules, we often care only about bond stretches (e.g., $C \equiv O$ in metal carbonyls).

Shortcut Basis: Bond Vectors

Instead of $3N$ coordinates, use only the arrows along the bonds as your basis.
$\chi_{stretch}(R)$ = Number of bonds that do not move.

This greatly simplifies the math for finding vibrational fingerprints in IR spectra.
