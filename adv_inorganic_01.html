Part 1: The Geometry of Electronic Structure

1. Prerequisite: The Hamiltonian

Quantum Mechanical Invariance

Molecular symmetry is not just about aesthetics; it defines the mathematical constraints of the Schrödinger equation. A symmetry operation $\hat{R}$ must commute with the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$.

$$ [\hat{H}, \hat{R}] = 0 \implies \hat{H}\hat{R}\psi = \hat{R}\hat{H}\psi $$

This leads to the vanishing integral theorem: if two states have different symmetries, their interaction energy is strictly zero.

2. Operation vs Element

Operation

A physical movement (rotation, reflection) that leaves the molecule in an indistinguishable state.

Element

The geometric reference (point, line, or plane) about which the operation is performed.

3. Identity ( $E$ )

The "do nothing" operation. Every molecule has at least this symmetry.

$$ \hat{E}\psi = \psi $$

Why is it needed? For a set of operations to form a mathematical Group, an identity element is mandatory (similar to '0' in addition or '1' in multiplication).

4. Proper Rotation ( $C_n$ )

Rotation by an angle $\theta = 360^\circ/n$ around an axis.

$C_2$ Axis

180° rotation.
Example: $H_2O$ (bisecting H-O-H angle).

$C_3$ Axis

120° rotation.
Example: $NH_3$ (perpendicular to the H3 plane).

Mathematical check: $C_n^n = E$

5. The Principal Axis

When multiple rotation axes exist, the one with the highest value of $n$ is the Principal Axis.

The z-axis Convention

By convention, the Principal Axis is assigned as the **z-axis** in the Cartesian coordinate system. This is crucial for interpreting Character Tables.

Example: Benzene ($C_6H_6$) has one $C_6$ and six $C_2$ axes. The $C_6$ is the principal axis.

6. Reflection ( $\sigma$ )

Reflection through a mirror plane. $\sigma^2 = E$.

$$ \sigma_{xy} (x, y, z) = (x, y, -z) $$
$\sigma_h$ (Horizontal)
Perp to Principal Axis
$\sigma_v$ (Vertical)
Contains Principal Axis
$\sigma_d$ (Dihedral)
Bisects $C_2$ axes

7. Inversion ( $i$ )

Reflection through a center of symmetry $(0,0,0)$.

$$ \hat{i} (x, y, z) = (-x, -y, -z) $$

Present in "Centrosymmetric" molecules such as $SF_6$, $CO_2$, or staggered Ethane.

8. Improper Rotation ( $S_n$ )

A two-step composite operation:
Rotation ($C_n$) followed by perpendicular Reflection ($\sigma_h$).

$$ \hat{S}_n = \hat{\sigma}_h \times \hat{C}_n $$

Important Distinction:

Neither $\sigma_h$ nor $C_n$ needs to be a symmetry operation on its own; only the result of the combined action must be.

9. $S_n$ Redundancies

Low-order Improper Rotations are equivalent to other fundamental operations:

  • $S_1$ Case

    $\hat{S}_1 = \hat{\sigma}_h \hat{C}_1 = \hat{\sigma} \times \hat{E} = \sigma$

  • $S_2$ Case

    $\hat{S}_2 = \hat{\sigma}_h \hat{C}_2 = i$ (The mathematical definition of Inversion)

10. $S_n$ Persistence

If $n$ is Odd

The existence of $S_n$ **requires** that both $C_n$ and $\sigma_h$ exist independently in the point group.

If $n$ is Even

The $S_n$ axis can exist **without** $C_n$ or $\sigma_h$ being present. Example: Methane ($CH_4$) has $S_4$ axes but no $C_4$.

11. Mathematical Group Axioms

The set of all symmetry operations of a molecule forms a Group under the operation of composition.

1. Closure: $A, B \in G \implies A \cdot B \in G$
2. Identity: $E \in G$ such that $A \cdot E = A$
3. Associativity: $(A \cdot B) \cdot C = A \cdot (B \cdot C)$
4. Inverse: For every $A \in G$, there exists $A^{-1}$ such that $A \cdot A^{-1} = E$

12. Point Groups Classification

We use **Schoenflies Notation** to categorize molecules into specific Point Groups.

$C_{nv}$
$D_{nh}$
$O_h$
$T_d$

"Point" refers to the fact that the center of mass remains fixed during all operations.

13. Low Symmetry Groups

$C_1$

Only Identity ($E$).
Asymmetric.

$C_s$

Only $E$ and one $\sigma$.

$C_i$

Only $E$ and Inversion ($i$).

14. High Symmetry (Platonic)

$T_d$ (Tetrahedral)

4 $C_3$ axes, 3 $C_2$ axes, 6 $\sigma_d$ planes.
Example: $CH_4, SiCl_4$

$O_h$ (Octahedral)

Highest symmetry for most complexes. Includes Inversion.
Example: $SF_6, [Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}$

15. Linear Molecules

Linear molecules possess an infinite rotation axis $C_\infty$.

$C_{\infty v}$

No Inversion.
Example: $HCl, CO, HCN$

$D_{\infty h}$

Has Inversion ($i$).
Example: $H_2, O_2, CO_2$

16. The C vs D Bifurcation

Does the molecule have $n$ perpendicular $C_2$ axes?
NO

C Family

Based solely on the Principal Axis $C_n$.

YES

D Family

"D" stands for Dihedral. High symmetry rotational set.

17. Decision Tree: Part 1

Q1

Is it Linear or Highly Symmetric?

Yes $\rightarrow$ $C_{\infty v}, D_{\infty h}, T_d, O_h, I_h$

Q2

Is there a Proper Rotation axis $C_n$ ($n > 1$)?

No $\rightarrow$ $C_s, C_i, C_1$

18. Decision Tree: Part 2

Are there $n$ $C_2$ axes perpendicular to $C_n$?

No $\rightarrow$ C Series

  • $\sigma_h$ present? $\rightarrow C_{nh}$
  • $n \sigma_v$ present? $\rightarrow C_{nv}$
  • Else $\rightarrow C_n$

Yes $\rightarrow$ D Series

  • $\sigma_h$ present? $\rightarrow D_{nh}$
  • $n \sigma_d$ present? $\rightarrow D_{nd}$
  • Else $\rightarrow D_n$

19. Symmetry & Polarity

A molecule is **Non-Polar** if its point group forces the dipole moment $\mu$ to be zero.

Symmetry Forbidden Polarity:

  • Any group with Inversion ($i$)
  • Any D group (has perp $C_2$ axes)
  • The high symmetry groups ($T_d, O_h$)

Only $C_1, C_s, C_n, C_{nv}$ groups can be polar.

20. Symmetry & Chirality

A molecule is Chiral if and only if it lacks any Improper Rotation axis ( $S_n$ ).

$S_1 = \sigma$

Mirror planes forbid chirality.

$S_2 = i$

Inversion centers forbid chirality.

21. Case Study: Water ( $H_2O$ )

Symmetry Elements

  • $E$ (Identity)
  • $C_2$ (Principal Axis)
  • $\sigma_v(xz)$ (Molecular plane)
  • $\sigma_v'(yz)$ (Bisecting plane)

Point Group: $C_{2v}$

Status: Polar, Achiral

22. Case Study: Ammonia ( $NH_3$ )

Symmetry Elements

  • $E$ (Identity)
  • $C_3$ (Principal Axis)
  • 3 $\sigma_v$ (Containing N-H bonds)

Point Group: $C_{3v}$

Status: Polar, Achiral

23. Case Study: Benzene ( $C_6H_6$ )

Symmetry Elements

  • $C_6$ (Principal Axis)
  • 6 $C_2$ (Perpendicular to $C_6$)
  • $\sigma_h$ (Molecular plane)
  • $i$ (Inversion center)

Point Group: $D_{6h}$

Status: Non-Polar, Achiral

24. Advanced Case: Allene ( $C_3H_4$ )

A classic example where $S_4$ exists but $C_4$ does not.

  • $C_2$ Principal Axis (thru central C)
  • 2 Perpendicular $C_2$ axes
  • 2 $\sigma_d$ planes
  • Has $S_4$ axis!

Point Group: $D_{2d}$

Status: Non-Polar, Achiral

25. Challenge: $XeF_4$

1. High Symmetry? No.

2. Principal Axis? $C_4$ (thru Xe).

3. 4 perp $C_2$? Yes (D-family).

4. $\sigma_h$ present? Yes (Molecular plane).

Result: $D_{4h}$

26. Transition: Matrix Forms

Every operation can be represented by a **$3 \times 3$ Transformation Matrix**.

$$ C_n(z) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The Trace (Sum of diagonals) of these matrices leads to the "Characters" used in Character Tables.
